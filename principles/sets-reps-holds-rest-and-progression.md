## What this page is

This page explains the words GymPrimer exercise pages use when they describe a beginner starting point: sets, repetitions, timed holds, effort, rest, and progression. It is general exercise literacy for healthy adults and returning beginners. [CDC][cdc-adult-activity]

## What this page is not

This page is not a personal prescription, medical advice, rehabilitation guidance, or a workout plan. It does not choose exercises, weekly frequency, load, or progression for one reader. [ACSM][acsm-resistance-training]

## Plain-language overview

A **repetition** is one complete movement, such as one chest press or one bodyweight row. A **set** is a small group of repetitions done before resting.

A **timed hold** is work measured by time instead of repetitions. A plank, stretch, or control drill may use a hold such as 10 to 30 seconds instead of a repetition count.

**Easy effort** means you could keep going with control. **Moderate effort** means the work is noticeable but still repeatable. **Hard effort** means you are close to losing clean form, steady breathing, or the movement path. Beginner pages should usually leave room for control rather than pushing to failure. [Mayo Clinic][mayo-weight-training]

**Rest** is the pause between sets, holds, or rounds. Rest is part of the method, not wasted time. It gives you a chance to breathe, reset setup, and keep the next set controlled.

**Progression** means making one small part of the exercise harder after the current version is repeatable. Change one variable at a time: add a repetition, add a few seconds, use a slightly harder variation, or make a small load increase. Do not change every variable at once.

Starter ranges on exercise pages are educational examples, not personal prescriptions. They help a beginner understand the shape of the exercise method. They are not a promise that the same amount is right for every body, goal, or medical context.

## General ranges

Dynamic resistance exercises often use sets and repetitions. A common beginner starting shape is a small number of controlled sets, a repetition range that can be done with clean form, rest between sets, and progression by adding repetitions before making a small load increase. [Mayo Clinic][mayo-weight-training] [ACSM][acsm-resistance-training]

Bodyweight progressions may use repetitions or time. The main idea is to choose a variation that stays controlled, then make the variation harder only after the current version is repeatable.

Low-load control drills and mobility drills should feel like practice. The goal is smoother control or comfortable range, not fatigue.

Isometric holds, stretches, and some mobility drills use time. A page may say to hold a position for a short duration, breathe normally, and stop or back off if posture, breathing, or comfort breaks down. [Mayo Clinic][mayo-stretching]

## How to use exercise-page ranges

Read the amount on an exercise page as a starting example for learning the movement. Keep the effort low enough that you can notice setup, range, breathing, and form.

Use the stop condition on the page. If the page says to stop when form, breathing, bracing, or comfort changes, that stop condition is part of the method. [Mayo Clinic][mayo-weight-training]

Use one-variable progression. For example, add a repetition before adding weight, add a few seconds before choosing a harder hold, or choose a slightly harder bodyweight variation before adding many more sets.

If symptoms, medical history, injury, or uncertainty are the main question, a static Markdown page is not enough. Use the central red-flags reference and qualified professional care when needed. [NHS Inform][nhs-inform-back-problems]
