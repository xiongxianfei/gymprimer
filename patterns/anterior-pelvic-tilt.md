## What this page is

This page explains anterior pelvic tilt as an observable pelvis-and-spine position: the front of the pelvis tips down and the low back may look more arched. [NASM][local-anterior-pelvic-tilt-nasm]

## What this page is not

This page does not diagnose the reader, prove that a posture is harmful, or provide individualized care.

## Red flags: when to stop reading and seek care

Read [the red-flags reference](../about/red-flags.md) before using any GymPrimer page to think about pain or training decisions.

## Plain-language overview

Anterior pelvic tilt is a posture description, not a verdict about a person. Some people show this position without symptoms, and some people with low back symptoms do not show it. [Physiopedia][local-anterior-pelvic-tilt-physiopedia]

## What mainstream sources generally agree on

Educational and professional sources commonly discuss hip-flexor length, trunk control, glute strength, and general movement habits as possible contributors to a tilted-pelvis posture. [NASM][local-anterior-pelvic-tilt-nasm]

Exercise pages that teach squatting, hinging, and bracing should help the reader notice pelvis and rib position without promising posture correction. [ACE][local-anterior-pelvic-tilt-ace]

## What is uncertain or mixed

Posture alone does not explain every person's pain or training limit. A visible pelvis position can be one clue for coaching, but it is not a stand-alone medical answer. [Physiopedia][local-anterior-pelvic-tilt-physiopedia]

## Commonly recommended self-management themes

Common education themes include learning neutral pelvis positions, practicing controlled trunk bracing, strengthening glutes and abdominals, and using hip-flexor mobility work as general training options. [NASM][local-anterior-pelvic-tilt-nasm]

These are themes to understand movement, not a routine selected for the reader.

## What to avoid

Avoid treating anterior pelvic tilt as a flaw that always causes pain. Avoid copying a routine from the internet when pain, nerve symptoms, major weakness, or medical history changes the situation.

## When to see a professional

Use a physical therapist, clinician, or qualified coach when pain limits normal activity, symptoms are recurring, or the reader cannot tell whether a movement is appropriate.
