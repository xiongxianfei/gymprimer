## What this is for

The rowing machine is cardio equipment with a skill component. Use it first to
learn a smooth stroke, then use that stroke for easy or moderate conditioning.

Five beginner use cases:

- Learn the basic rowing stroke without treating the machine as a pull-as-hard
  contest.
- Practice a repeatable foot, seat, handle, and posture setup.
- Build easy aerobic work with a low-impact machine.
- Use short rounds to learn how stroke rhythm and effort feel.
- Add simple conditioning while still keeping separate strength work in the
  week.

## Before you start

Start easy and focus on technique before conditioning. Concept2 tells beginners
not to pull too hard until they are comfortable with the technique fundamentals.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

Set the damper to a middle feel while learning. The damper changes the feel of
the flywheel; it is not a score for how much work you did. You create the work
with your stroke effort. [Concept2][concept2-damper-setting]

## Equipment setup

Sit on the seat, place your feet on the footplates, and tighten the straps so
each foot strap crosses the ball or widest part of the foot.
[Concept2][concept2-foot-position]

Hold the handle with a relaxed grip. At the catch, keep the arms long, head
neutral, shoulders low, and spine long. Shins approach vertical only as far as
comfortable; do not force extra range to reach the front of the slide.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

The handle should travel in a straight path toward and away from the flywheel.
Keep the recovery controlled instead of rushing back to the front.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

## Muscles involved

The drive should start with the legs and glutes. The trunk helps keep posture
and transfer force through the stroke. The upper back, lats, and arms help
finish the handle pull near the ribs. Treat this as broad exercise literacy,
not a precise muscle-activation test. [Concept2][local-rowing-machine-muscles]

## Movement breakdown

Core sequence:

```text
Drive: legs -> body -> arms
Recovery: arms -> body -> legs
```

Concept2 describes the drive as leg press first, then body swing, then arm
pull. The recovery reverses that by sending the arms away, hinging from the
hips, and then bending the knees. [Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

### 1. Catch

Start with arms straight, shoulders low, torso leaning forward from the hips,
and shins close to vertical as comfort allows.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

### 2. Drive

Press with the legs first. As the legs extend, swing the torso through tall.
Finish by pulling the handle toward the lower ribs.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

### 3. Finish

End with legs extended, torso leaning back slightly, shoulders low, wrists flat,
and the handle held lightly below the ribs.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

### 4. Recovery

Send the arms away first. Then hinge forward from the hips. When the hands have
cleared the knees, let the knees bend and slide forward under control.
[Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

## What you should feel

You should feel a smooth leg-driven push, a steady trunk position, and a light
upper-back and arm finish. The stroke should feel connected rather than jerky.

Breathing should feel like easy or moderate cardio during beginner practice. If
the stroke becomes rushed, noisy, or painful, make the effort easier and reset
your technique. [Concept2][concept2-damper-setting]

## Common mistakes

- Starting the drive by yanking with the arms.
- Leaning back before the legs have started the drive.
- Bending the knees before the hands clear them on the recovery.
- Setting the foot straps too high or too low for a comfortable catch.
- Treating the damper as a difficulty score to max out.
- Letting the recovery rush faster than the drive.

## How much to do

Method type: basic_cardio_equipment

Beginner starting point: Start with 3-5 minutes of easy rowing. Take a break to
walk, breathe, or reset technique. If you still feel good, repeat up to four
short rounds. [Concept2][concept2-getting-started-workouts]

Effort: Keep the first practice easy or moderate. Learn the stroke before
trying harder pace or higher power. [Concept2][concept2-getting-started-workouts]

Rest/reset: Rest long enough that your next round starts smooth again. Use the
break to check foot straps, posture, and the drive/recovery sequence.
[Concept2][concept2-getting-started-workouts]

Progression: Technique first, then time, then moderate effort. Make the stroke
smoother before making the session longer. Add moderate effort only after the
sequence stays controlled. [Concept2][concept2-getting-started-workouts]

Stop condition: Stop the round if technique becomes painful, jerky or uncontrolled. [Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]
Use the central
[Red Flags](../RED-FLAGS.md) page for symptoms that need more than exercise
education. [Concept2][concept2-rowing-technique]

Static examples:

- 3-5 minutes easy x 1-4 rounds for first exposure.
- 10-15 minutes easy steady rowing for simple conditioning.
- 3 minutes easy or moderate with 1 minute rest for learning effort changes.
- 15 minutes steady rowing when the stroke stays smooth.

These are examples for learning and conditioning, not a schedule or race plan.

Rowing can contribute to aerobic activity, but it does not replace all strength
training. Adult activity guidance includes aerobic activity and
muscle-strengthening activity on at least two days per week.
[CDC][cdc-adult-activity] [WHO][who-physical-activity]

## Easier version

Use fewer minutes, lighter effort, and a lower stroke rate. Pause after each
short round and restart only when the sequence feels organized.

## Harder version

Keep the same damper and make the stroke smoother for longer. Then add time.
Only after that, use moderate effort while keeping the same sequence.

## Safety notes

Stop rowing and use the central [Red Flags](../RED-FLAGS.md) page if you notice chest pain, dizziness, fainting, or unusual shortness of breath. [Mayo Clinic][local-rowing-machine-safety]

Stop for sharp pain, symptoms that worsen, or numbness. Use the Red Flags page instead of trying to row through those symptoms. [Mayo Clinic][local-rowing-machine-exercise-pain] [NHS][nhs-back-pain]

Stop the round if technique becomes painful or the movement turns jerky or uncontrolled. Proper technique should stay controlled, and exercise that causes pain should stop. [Mayo Clinic][mayo-weight-training]
