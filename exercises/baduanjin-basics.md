## What this is for

Baduanjin Basics is gentle qigong movement for posture and breathing practice, coordination, body-awareness, and slow movement literacy. NCCIH describes qigong as involving body movement and posture, breath regulation, and attention, and it names Baduanjin as one qigong form. [NCCIH][nccih-qigong]

This page teaches only a short beginner introduction: ready stance, two hands lift upward, drawing the bow, alternating reach, and return to quiet standing. The written Markdown remains the source of truth for setup, muscles, method, and safety.

## Before you start

Practice on a clear, flat surface. Wear comfortable clothing and use shoes, socks, or bare feet only when the foot contact is non-slip. Keep movements small at first, and keep a wall or stable chair nearby when balance is uncertain. VA describes Tai Chi and Qigong as gentle practices using slow movements, breathing, and focus, and notes that qigong combines breathing, posture, focus, and slow movement. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]

Ask a qualified instructor or clinician before using this page when a medical condition, medication, injury history, dizziness, or balance concern makes gentle standing practice uncertain. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]

## Setup

Stand tall on a clear, flat surface. Place the feet at a comfortable width, soften the knees, keep the trunk upright, relax the shoulders, and let the arms rest naturally.

Keep the first version small. Let the feet feel connected to the floor, breathe normally, and stay close enough to a wall or stable chair to pause or steady the body if needed. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]

## Muscles involved

| Role | Muscle region | What it helps do |
|---|---|---|
| Support and weight shift | Legs and glutes | Help you stay grounded and move weight slowly. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong] |
| Posture and balance | Trunk | Helps keep the body tall while the arms move. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong] |
| Arm motion | Shoulders and upper back | Help the arms rise, open, and lower without shrugging. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong] |
| Foot control | Feet and ankles | Help you feel the ground and make small balance adjustments. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong] |

Treat this as broad body-awareness guidance, not a test of exact muscle effort.

## Movement breakdown

### 1. Ready stance

Stand in the setup position. Let the knees stay soft, the shoulders settle, and the breath stay quiet. [NCCIH][nccih-qigong]

### 2. Two hands lift upward

Let the hands rise slowly in front of the body toward a comfortable overhead or near-overhead range. Keep the trunk long, the knees soft, and the shoulders relaxed instead of shrugged. [NCCIH][nccih-qigong]

### 3. Drawing the bow

Step or shift into a comfortable side stance. Open one arm outward while the other arm draws back, as if making a simple bow shape, without forcing the shoulders, wrists, neck, or knees. [Baduanjin review][baduanjin-review]

### 4. Alternating reach

Let one hand reach upward while the other lowers gently. Keep the movement smooth and comfortable, and keep the trunk upright rather than leaning to chase more range. [Baduanjin review][baduanjin-review]

### 5. Return to quiet standing

Bring the body back to relaxed standing. Let the arms rest naturally and breathe normally before starting another short round.

## What you should feel

You may feel quiet work in the legs and glutes as weight shifts and the knees stay soft. Pay attention to a tall trunk, relaxed shoulders, steady feet, and breathing that stays normal.

Try to keep the movement smooth enough that the arms feel light, the stance stays comfortable, and the body can return to quiet standing without rushing.

## Common mistakes

- Locking the knees at the start.
- Making the stance deep before the movement feels smooth.
- Raising the arms until the shoulders shrug.
- Turning the head, trunk, or shoulders farther than feels comfortable.
- Moving quickly instead of slowly enough to notice balance and breathing.
- Trying to make this short beginner page into a long sequence.

## How much to do

Method type: low_load_control_drill

Beginner starting point: Practice for 3-5 minutes as static general education, using small slow movement and normal breathing. [NCCIH][nccih-qigong] [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]
Effort: Keep the effort easy enough that posture, breathing, balance, and attention stay calm.
Rest: Rest as needed between short practice rounds.
Progression: First make the movement smoother. Then practice for a little longer. Do not make the stance deeper or the range larger just to make it harder. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]
Stop if: Stop if balance, posture, breathing, or attention breaks down. Stop for dizziness, chest pain, fainting, unusual shortness of breath, sharp pain, numbness, weakness, worsening symptoms, loss of balance control, or uncertainty caused by medical condition, medication, injury history, or balance concerns. Use the central [Red Flags](../RED-FLAGS.md) page when symptoms need more than exercise education. [Mayo Clinic][local-baduanjin-basics-red-flags] [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]

These are general starter labels for a low-load control drill, not a personal schedule.

## Easier version

Make each movement smaller. Keep both feet fully on the floor and stay close to a wall or stable chair. Seated or instructor-guided options may be more appropriate when standing practice is uncertain. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]

## Harder version

Use the same short pattern for a little longer while keeping the knees soft, the trunk upright, the shoulders relaxed, and the breathing normal. Add smoothness before size, speed, depth, or more movements.

## Safety notes

Stop and seek appropriate help for chest pain, fainting, severe dizziness, unusual shortness of breath, sharp pain, numbness, weakness, worsening symptoms, loss of balance control, or any feeling that the movement is unsafe. Use [Red Flags](../RED-FLAGS.md) for the central safety route. [Mayo Clinic][local-baduanjin-basics-red-flags]

Use a wall or stable chair if balance is uncertain. Keep the first version small, avoid deep knee bending, and avoid forcing neck, shoulder, back, or hamstring range. Ask a qualified instructor or clinician when a medical condition, medication, injury history, dizziness, or balance concern makes practice uncertain. [VA][va-tai-chi-qigong]
